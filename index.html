Preparing for the Digital SAT

The SAT is changing: starting from 2023, students outside of the US will be taking the new Digital SAT. The change has already taken place for students taking the test outside the United States. Don't worry. This book will teach you everything you need to know to ace the test.

Whereas the previous versions of the SAT were divided into clearly marked Reading and Writing sections, the new Digital version has merged the two into one section. Yet, the distinction is still easy to see.

The first half of the test is the Reading questions; the remaining half is the Writing questions.

Anatomy of the Digital SAT Writing Questions

There are three main types of Digital SAT Writing Questions:

  1. Grammar questions
  2. Transition questions
  3. Listing / notes questions

Grammar questions simply test different grammar concepts. There are 12 grammar concepts to remember.

Transition questions require us to identify the logical relationship—such as contrast, emphasis, time, and cause & effect—between two sentences.

Listing / notes questions check our grammatical ability to create a concise sentence while answering the given question. This is very similar to the effectively combined questions from the previous SAT.

How To Approach Grammar Questions

"Approach a grammar question in the same way as you approach a simple math question."

When solving simple math questions, we do not need the answer choices.

4 × 2 = ?

a) 5    b) 6    c) 7    d) 8

We all know the answer to this question without looking at the answer choices. It should be exactly the same for the grammar test.

After learning all the grammar concepts and rules taught in this book, you will be able to solve grammar questions without looking at any answer choices.

Many of us have misconceptions about English that prevent us from achieving greater success in the SAT, so the first thing to do is to eliminate these misconceptions.

Study the fundamentals on the next page, and RESET your knowledge of the English language.

The Six Fundamentals

Fundamental 1: Every sentence has a subject and a verb.

A sentence MUST have at least two words, but they cannot be any two words. A sentence must always have a Subject (the main character of a sentence) and a Verb.

In one sentence, there can be 1 subject or 20 subjects; the number doesn't matter.

The ticket for the movie that my daughter wanted to see was cheap.

S1                         S2    V2    V1

What matters is this one simple rule: a SUBJECT is, in essence, always a NOUN

Fundamental 2: A subject (noun) is not limited to one word.

A noun is a person, place, object, or anything that has a "NAME". However, it can be more than one word.

Example: What I wanted was you.

The subject of this sentence is "What I wanted."

Fundamental 3: Anything that is not a sentence is a MODIFIER.

Fundamentally, everything we write is either a sentence or a modifier (i.e., incomplete sentence). Forget all the fancy terms (e.g., dependent clause or participle phrase). Simplify everything. A modifier adds more information and detail to the sentence, and there are two basic types we need to know: Adjective and Adverb.

Adjectives give more information about nouns, whereas Adverbs give more information about everything else, including the verb, sentence, and other modifiers.

Fundamental 4: MODIFIERS (adjective and adverb) can also be more than one word.

In fact, they come in various forms and lengths. Below are some examples.

Different Forms of Adjective

1. I have a new car. (1 word adjective)

2. I have a car that is fast. (adjective clause)

3. I have a car, a gift from my father. (appositive)

4. I have a car made in Italy. (participle phrase)

Different Forms of Adverb

1. I sang loudly. (1 word adverb)

2. When I was young, I liked her. (adverb clause)

3. Yesterday, I sang. (noun adverb)

4. I studied in the morning. (adverb prepositional phrase)

Fundamental 5: The same word can have a different function in a sentence.

Yesterday was fun.   (subject/noun)

Yesterday, I sang.   (modifier/adverb)

Red is my favorite color.   (subject/noun)

I have a red car.   (modifier/adjective)

Where he went last night was the library.   (subject / noun)

I would love to visit Paris where people are romantic.   (modifier/adjective)

Where there is life, there is hope.   (modifier/adverb)

Fundamental 6: DO NOT ALWAYS ERASE the information between two commas.

Understand that in one sentence, there can be many different comma usages.

It is true that often, the idea between two commas is a non-essential modifier.

Ferrari, one of the most expensive cars in the world, is very popular.

However, we have to understand that the information between two commas is NOT ALWAYS non-essential (removable).

Do not automatically assume. Read the sentence and check how commas are used.

Example: Yesterday, I went to see her, even though my friends advised me not to.

There are two commas here because one is used to separate the transition "yesterday" from the main sentence, while the other is used to separate the sentence and the modifier beginning with "even though."

Concept 1: Punctuation

Punctuation is one of the most important concepts to learn for the SAT because it is tested most often.

Punctuation refers to using different marks, such as a period, comma, and colon, to clarify meaning and provide better communication. In other words, punctuation is used to make the meaning of sentences clearer.

Since its first usage, the English language has evolved, and punctuation is no exception. Studying punctuation is difficult because each punctuation mark has numerous usages. Also, because some writers deliberately break or manipulate punctuation rules to create a specific mood or emphasize certain ideas, punctuation can be confusing. But don't worry.

The SAT is a standardized test, following set rules, and this book will teach you everything you need to know about SAT Punctuation.

Parallel Punctuation and Comma Splice

Concept 1.1: Parallel Punctuation

Parallel Punctuation is arguably one of the most important punctuation concepts tested in the SAT because it composes the majority of easy and difficult punctuation questions.

The rule is simple:

Parallel Punctuation Rule 1

There cannot be just 1 comma between a subject and a verb.

Dan, who loves Jane lives in Canada.

S   ,                 V

Between a subject and a verb, there should be 0 or 2 commas.

Dan who loves Jane lives in Canada.

S                 V

Dan, who loves Jane, lives in Canada.

S   ,              ,   V

*Understand that anything that is between the subject and the verb (or anything that is not the core element of a sentence) is called a non-essential MODIFIER.

Dan, who loves Jane, lives in Canada.

S ,     modifier     , V

Parallel Punctuation Rule 2

The punctuation marks surrounding the non-essential modifier must be parallel.

Dan, who loves Jane—lives in Canada.   (S , —V)

Dan, who loves Jane, lives in Canada.

S ,                  , V

When we are separating non-essential modifiers, there are 3 ways:

1. Comma

Hamlet, a play written by Shakespeare, is a masterpiece.

I recently read Hamlet, a play written by Shakespeare.

*Notice how only one comma is used when it comes at the end of a sentence.

2. Dash

Hamlet—a play written by Shakespeare—is a masterpiece.

I recently read Hamlet—a play written by Shakespeare.

*The dash usage in this way is the same as the comma usage above. The dash has other uses as well, which will be discussed later.

3. Parentheses (round brackets)

Hamlet (a play written by Shakespeare) is a masterpiece.

I recently read Hamlet (a play written by Shakespeare).

*With parentheses, even when a modifier comes at the end, another must be used to close it. Also, note that the period comes after the parentheses.

Parallel Punctuation Rule 3

Within the core element of a sentence, the modifier(s) must be clearly punctuated.

A. There cannot be just one comma within a verb.

Sometimes, a verb is made up of 2 words or more (i.e., Verb phrase). In this case, there cannot be just one comma within a verb.

I have , studied.

Within a verb, there should be 0 or 2 commas.

I have studied.

I have, by the way studied.

I have, by the way, studied.

B. There cannot be just one comma between a verb and an object.

I invented, the device.   (V , O)

Between a verb and an object, there should be 0 or 2 commas.

I invented the device.   (V   O)

I invented, despite his disapproval, the device.   (V ,                   , O)

Concept 1.2: Comma Splice / Run-on Sentence

Along with Parallel Punctuation, a comma splice, also known as a run-on sentence, is the most common error tested in the SAT.

A comma splice error occurs when two complete sentences are joined only by a comma.

I love Jane, she hates me.

Chelsea F.C. is a soccer team, the L.A. Clippers is a basketball team.

There are various ways of fixing a comma splice. However, there are 2 fundamental methods.

1st Method: Change the Punctuation Mark

This is the easiest method of fixing a comma splice. If a comma is the problem, all we have to do is change the comma. Within the 1st method, there are 5 grammatical ways:

  1. Period
  2. Semicolon
  3. Comma + Main Conjunction (FANBOYS)
  4. Colon
  5. Dash

1–2. Period & Semicolon

They are grammatically the same. The only difference is the capitalization.

Many movies are entertaining. The movies from Marvel are especially entertaining.

Many movies are entertaining; the movies from Marvel are especially entertaining.

3. Comma + Main Conjunction (FANBOYS)

For, And, Nor, But, Or, Yet, So can connect two independent sentences.

I ran home, and she stayed in school.

I wanted to go home, so I got on the bus.

One mistake that students make the most is using HOWEVER to connect two sentences.

I called him, however, he did not answer.

The word "however" is not FANBOYS and does not have the power to connect two sentences. This error can be simply fixed by:

I called him; however, he did not answer.

I called him. However, he did not answer.

Or by replacing "however" with a contrasting conjunction from FANBOYS:

I called him, but he did not answer.

I called him, yet he did not answer.

It is perfectly fine to use the word "however" this way, though:

I, however, was wrong. (Here, "however" is not connecting two sentences.)

4–5. Colon and Dash

Colon is used when the 2nd sentence elaborates the first sentence.

I passed the test: the test was easy.

Dash, which will be covered later, has many usages, and it can also connect two sentences.

The rule is simple—you need to pass the test.

Again, these 5 methods are grammatical ways of fixing comma splices. Some create different meanings, so it is important to understand each punctuation.

2nd Method: Change or Kill the Sentence

Sometimes, it's not possible to change the comma because the option is not given or the given punctuation option is used incorrectly. In this case, we have to change the sentence into a modifier.

Every night, he partied and played with his roommates, he had a math exam in two days. It seemed clear to his parents that he didn't care about his study.

A) No change
B) although he had
C) because he had
D) however, he had

We cannot change the comma as it is not underlined. The only way to fix the error is to change one sentence into a modifier with the correct relationship. Answer choice D ('however') is a comma splice. The correct answer is B "although he had".

As they roamed the battle field, the generals held up their banners, each banner was representing a house. The field was silent.

A) No change
B) represented
C) had represented
D) representing

Answer choice D "representing" removes the verb and changes the sentence into a modifier, fixing the comma splice error.

Fixing Comma Splice — Summary

1st — Change the punctuation mark

  1. Period
  2. Semicolon
  3. Comma + FANBOYS
  4. Colon (elaboration)
  5. Dash (elaboration)

2nd — Change the Sentence into a modifier

I like her, she is kind.

I like her because she is kind.

I sold my books, the books were gifts.

I sold my books, which were gifts.

He studied, He had to pass the test.

He studied to pass the test.

Concept 1.3: Colon vs. Semicolon

Both a colon and a semicolon must follow a complete sentence. When using a semicolon, the second part also has to be a complete sentence. However, with a colon, as long as the first part is a sentence, anything can follow.

Sentence ; sentence
Sentence: anything

Semicolon

A semicolon is used like a period. It does not add anything nor change the meaning of a sentence. The only difference is the capitalization of the second sentence.

Many movies are entertaining. The movies from Marvel are especially entertaining.

Many movies are entertaining; the movies from Marvel are especially entertaining.

*One common mistake that students make is using "semicolon + FANBOYS." Remember, we do not use a semicolon with FANBOYS (with the exception of super-comma which will be discussed later).

I saw Jane; and I knew at that moment.

I saw Jane, and I knew at that moment.

I saw Jane and knew at that moment. (correct / parallelism)

I saw Jane. I knew at that moment.

Colon

A colon can be used in two ways:

  1. Before an elaboration / add more information or give detail
  2. Before a list
List

I covered many of the fundamentals in our class: grammar, punctuation, and vocabulary.

Sir Alex Ferguson discovered many superstars: David Beckham, Paul Scholes, and Cristiano Ronaldo.

*Notice how it is possible to use an incomplete sentence after a colon as long as the first part of the information before the colon is a complete sentence.

Elaboration

I don't like Steve: he is selfish.

I need more time: I didn't finish my homework.

Important tip to remember: A colon cannot be used with words like "such as" or "including" because of REDUNDANCY.

I have three things: including A, B, and C.

When listing, use only one of the following:

I have three things: A, B, and C.

I have three things, including A, B, and C.

I have three things, such as A, B, and C.

Semicolon 2nd Usage — The Super-Comma

A semicolon has one more usage, though this is rarely tested in the SAT. The second usage is what I call "the super-comma."

Consider: I went to Seoul, Korea, Paris, France, and Hawaii.

How many places did I visit? For students who know geography well, the answer is three (Seoul, Paris, and Hawaii). However, for people who are unfamiliar with places, it could be read as FIVE completely different places.

To prevent this type of misunderstanding within a list, a semicolon can be used like a super-comma:

I went to Seoul, Korea; Paris, France; and Hawaii.

In this case, the semicolon can be used even if the phrases between it are incomplete sentences.

Concept 1.4: Comma

A comma is without a doubt the hardest punctuation in English because there are so many different usages.

Understand that in one sentence, there can be many different comma usages. More importantly, DO NOT ALWAYS ERASE the information between two commas.

Example 1: Unfortunately, he still liked her, even though she had rejected him.

Erasing 'he still liked her' wouldn't make any sense. There are two commas here because one is used to separate the transition "unfortunately" from the main sentence, while the other is used to separate the sentence and the modifier beginning with "even though."

Example 2: I bought some apples, grapes, and pears.

The commas here are used to list. We cannot cross out one item.

Basic Comma Usages

1. To separate an adverb/modifier before a sentence

Yesterday, I sang a song.

In the morning, I sang a song.

When I was young, I cried a lot.

Although he was happy, he wanted more.

No comma is generally used when an adverb/modifier is used after a sentence:

I sang a song, in the morning.

I sang a song in the morning.

I like her, because she is kind.

I like her because she is kind.

However, a comma is used when the modifier at the end of the sentence is a CONTRAST:

I like Jane, not Jenny.

I like to work fast, while Mike likes to work slowly.

2. To separate a transition from a sentence

Transition, IC    /    IC, transition    /    S, transition, V

However, I was wrong.

I was wrong, however.

I, however, was wrong.

Used correctly, transitions can come in the beginning, middle, and end. The three sentences above have the same meaning.

3. To connect two sentences with FANBOYS

Formula: Sentence, FANBOYS sentence

I went to see her, but she was not home.

I went to see her but she was not home.

4. To list (more than 3)

I bought a Ferrari, a Lamborghini, and a Rolls-Royce.

I play, sing, and dance.

5. To separate a non-essential modifier

(Arguably the most important usage in the SAT and will be discussed more.) In this case, you can erase the modifier.

Example 1: Jack, a boy from Hong Kong, is generous.

Example 2: I know Jack, a boy from Hong Kong.

*It is never about the number of commas that turns the information into a modifier. I can still erase 'a boy from Hong Kong' in example 2, even though there is only one comma.

6. To separate adjectives when order doesn't matter

It was a long, cold winter.

It was a cold, long winter.

In this case, adding "and" without a comma is also possible: It was a cold and long winter.

However, when the order is important, it doesn't work:

I have a white, cotton shirt.

I have a white cotton shirt.

Comma Summary Exercise

I knew, a brave, bold soldier.

Question 1: What is wrong with this sentence?

Question 2: How many commas do you see within the core element of the sentence (Subject, Verb, and Object)?

Answer to Question 1: No, because this sentence breaks the Parallel Punctuation rule.

Answer to Question 2: 1 comma.

In this sentence, we VISUALLY see 2 commas within the core elements (Verb and Object): I knew, a brave, bold soldier. However, the second comma between "brave" and "bold" is used to separate two adjectives with which the order is not important. In essence, this sentence has "one unnecessary" comma, violating the Parallel Punctuation rule.

I knew, a brave, bold soldier. (V , O)

I knew a brave, bold soldier. (V O)

Identifying Different Comma Usages

Example 1:

When I was young, I liked to sing, dance, and play with Jane, a girl from Canada.

In this sentence, there are three different usages of commas:

When I was young, = Adverb, sentence

to sing, dance, and play = listing commas

with Jane, a girl from Canada = non-essential modifier

Example 2:

All over the world, Shakespeare's plays, such as Othello and Macbeth, are still popular.

adverb, sentence     /     non-essential modifier

Exercise — Identify Different Comma Usages

1. Mr. Jack, who wanted to change the world, invented a creative, effective device.

2. To understand the world better, he went looking for the sage, even though he had no idea where to find him.

3. The girl, however, was interested in math, science, and history.

Miscellaneous Comma Rules

1. Comma is not used after a preposition (within a prepositional phrase).

I have a car from, Italy.

I have a car from Italy.

2. Comma is not used between an adjective and a noun.

I have a blue, car.

I have a blue car.

3. Comma is not used between an occupation and name.

President, Obama is the 44th president.

President Obama is the 44th president.

*Using a modifier between a name and occupation is a different case: Barack Obama, the former president, is actually the 43rd man to serve as a president.

4. Comma is not GENERALLY used BETWEEN a form and a name.

The tv show, Big Bang Theory is fun. (form) (name)

The tv show Big Bang Theory is fun.

Again, the rule is different when the order is reversed:

Big Bang Theory, the tv show, is fun.

Jane Eyre, the novel by Austen, is fun.

5. If used correctly, a comma CAN be used before "preposition" and the word "that"

As mentioned earlier, a comma is generally not used before a preposition. Also, a modifier beginning with "that" (essential modifier) is used without a comma:

I bought the book, that was controversial.

I bought the book that was controversial.

However, this is not always the case because it is possible to have different commas within one sentence:

I bought the book, despite his criticism, that was controversial.

In this case, it is perfectly fine to use a comma before the word "that" because this comma is used to separate the modifier/transition "despite his criticism."

Despite his criticism, I bought the book that was controversial.

Likewise:

I met Jane, a girl from Canada, in the morning.

Jane, in her own way, is looking for meaning in life.

6. Using "such as"

There are only 2 ways of using the phrase "such as":

, such as A, B, and C
such as A, B, and C

The first usage is for adding non-essential information (separating non-essential modifiers with a comma). The second usage is for adding essential information.

We cannot use a comma or a colon between "such" and "as":

such, as A, B, and C

such as, A, B, and C

We also cannot use a colon (:) and 'such as' together because of REDUNDANCY:

I have three things: such as A, B, and C.

I have three things: A, B, and C.

I have three things, such as A, B, and C.

I have three things such as A, B, and C.

7. Quotation

No comma is needed when the quote is not a complete sentence and is part of the sentence:

He said that he would "find the man."

The author believes watching TV is "beneficial" for students.

However, when a complete sentence / idea is quoted, a comma is used after the verb:

The headline in the newspaper declared, "People in tears, Embracing the change."

He said, "I will find the man."

Colon can also be used to quote more than one sentence (a sentence must be used before a colon):

Perhaps Buddha said it best: "Do not dwell in the past, do not dream of the future, and concentrate the mind on the present moment."

Concept 1.5: Non-Essential and Essential Modifier

Through commas, modifiers can become Essential or Non-Essential.

While essential modifiers are used without commas, non-essential modifiers are always used with commas.

If a sentence still makes perfect sense without certain modifiers, those modifiers are considered "non-essential" and are/will be surrounded by commas.

Othello, which explores the theme of jealousy, is Shakespeare's masterpiece.

It is also possible to use dashes or parentheses instead of commas:

Othello—which explores the theme of jealousy—is Shakespeare's masterpiece.

Othello (which explores the theme of jealousy) is Shakespeare's masterpiece.

It is perfectly fine to erase the non-essential modifier as it will still retain the same meaning.

If a non-essential modifier comes at the end of the sentence, only one comma is required:

I recently read Othello, which is Shakespeare's masterpiece.

It is also possible to make the modifier more concise:

I recently read Othello, Shakespeare's masterpiece.

With parentheses, even when a modifier comes at the end, another must be used to close it:

I recently read Othello (Shakespeare's masterpiece).

Yet, we cannot always use non-essential modifiers. Sometimes, essential modifiers are necessary. For example, writing a sentence like the one below is not recommended:

The painter, who drew the Mona Lisa, is a genius.

This is because, though it works grammatically, it is unclear who the painter refers to. In this case, we should use an essential modifier without a comma to make the meaning clear:

The painter who drew Mona Lisa is a genius.

Choosing between an essential modifier and non-essential modifier: Among thousands of punctuation questions tested in the SAT, only about 10 of them required students to choose between an essential modifier and a non-essential modifier. When we get to Concept 12, we will discuss this in detail.

Brian—a boy from Canada, is very smart.

An apostrophe is a punctuation mark that can be used to show possession or omit words.

1. Omission

He's studying for the test. = He is studying for the test.

2. Possession

Actors' hands are different.

People's ideas have changed over time.

Concept 1.8: Comma Splice Part 2

Now that we understand the basics of different punctuation marks, let's learn more about comma splice / run-on sentence errors.

"However" and Similar Words

Again, remember the word "however" is not FANBOYS and does not have the power to connect two sentences. Likewise, words like "Nevertheless, Even so, Consequently, Therefore" create a comma splice error when they are not punctuated properly.

I called him, however, he did not answer.

I called him, nevertheless, he did not answer.

I called him, even so, he did not answer.

I called him, consequently, he called me back.

I called him, therefore, he called me back.

Fix these by using a semicolon or period before the transition word:

I called him; however, he did not answer.

I called him. However, he did not answer.

I, however, was wrong. (Here "however" is not connecting two sentences — this is correct.)

Concept 1.9: Comma Splice Part 3

Identifying a comma splice error will allow us to eliminate most answer choices in punctuation questions. However, this is not as easy as it sounds. The fundamental reason is that there are so many different types of complete sentences.

1. Normal Sentence

I played with my friends.

The test by Dan is not that difficult.

We need to understand that words such as "some," "each," "both," "either," and "neither" can be used alone as the subject of a sentence and form a complete sentence.

Some were reluctant to raise the issue.

Each brought good news.

Either is fine.

words that begin with —wh are generally modifiers: who, which, whom, where, whose

2. Inverted Sentence

The word "inverted" means to reverse the position of something. In an inverted sentence, the order of the subject and the verb is inverted. The most common example:

"There is a boy." (verb → subject)

3. Imperative Sentence

Imperative sentences are used to make commands or instructions. The subject is the word "you," which is hidden.

(You) Hold my hand.

(You) Take this.

Below is an example of an imperative sentence used in a punctuation question:

Consider the effect of taking a one hour nap, for example, when people sleep for an hour during lunch, they tend to concentrate better throughout the day.

This is a comma splice — the imperative sentence is followed by another complete sentence. Fix:

Consider the effect of taking a one hour nap, for example; when people sleep for an hour during lunch, they tend to concentrate better throughout the day.

4. Shakespearean Sentence

This type of sentence is also tested in the SAT punctuation question. It is used in Shakespeare's plays:

Enter Romeo   (sentence)

Enter Juliet   (sentence)

Enter Romeo, he is one of the most tragic characters in literature.

Concept 1.10: Parallel Punctuation Part 2 (Advanced)

This section will deal with advanced Parallel Punctuation concepts, which comprises the vast majority of difficult grammar questions.

Basic Parallel Punctuation Rule: There cannot be just 1 comma between a subject and a verb. There should always be 0 or 2 commas within the core.

Advanced Parallel Punctuation Rule: Modifiers must be clearly punctuated within a LIST.

Simple Rules of Listing

When listing 2 people or 2 things, use "AND/OR/BUT" without a comma.
Formula: (A and B)   (A or B)   (A but B)

I sing or dance.

I sing, or dance. (A or B)

She called her mom and dad.

She called her mom, and dad. (A and B)

She is kind but mean.

She is kind, but mean. (A but B)

When listing 3 or more people/things, use "comma + AND/OR" at the end of the list.
Formula: (A, B, and C)   (A, B, C, and D)

I have a car, boat, and plane. (A, B, and C)

I have a car, boat and plane.

I sing, dance, run, and play. (A, B, C, and D)

I sing, dance, run and play.

Important Reminder: Understand that ,and is also used to connect two complete sentences. Develop the habit of identifying how ",and" is used in a sentence. When solving difficult punctuation questions, ask yourself whether the writer is listing or connecting two sentences.

Agreement refers to a situation in which people have the same opinion and feeling. In grammar, agreement refers to words having the same number of things.

There are 3 types of Agreement tested in the SAT:

  2. Noun Agreement
  3. Pronoun Agreement

Regardless of their names, three agreement questions test the same concept: a student's ability to identify and fix the number problem.

Concept 2: Subject-Verb Agreement

These countable nouns also come in two different forms: singular (1) and plural (2 or more). To make a plural form of a noun, we simply add —s or —es at the end of the word: friend (1) → friends (2 or more).

Verbs, on the other hand, have different characteristics. When writing in a present tense, verbs also have singular and plural forms, but the rule is the opposite: adding —s or —es at the end makes the verb "singular."

plays (1) → play (2 or more)

Singular Noun Singular Verb Plural Noun Plural Verb
Friend Plays Friends Play
Student Studies Students Study

The basic rule: When writing in present tense, use a singular verb with a singular subject, and use a plural verb with a plural subject.

BE VERB

Many students don't understand that words like "am," "are," and "is" are actually verbs — the "be verbs." They are unique because they come in many different forms and require subject-verb-agreement rules in both present and past tense.

Person Singular Present Singular Past Plural Present Plural Past
1st I am I was We are We were
2nd You are You were You are You were
3rd He, She, It is He, She, It was They are They were

To solve SVA questions well, develop the habit of eliminating modifiers and leaving only the "essential" or "core" element in a sentence.

The attempt / to kill the king / by the duke / failed.
Core sentence: The attempt failed.

The boy / who came from Canada / to meet his brothers / was happy.
Core sentence: The boy was happy.

Concept 2.1: Subject-Verb Agreement Rules

1. Singular subject takes singular verb.

My student lives in the city.

2. Plural subject takes plural verb.

My students live in the city.

3. Uncountable subject takes singular verb.

Water is refreshing.

4. Past tense ignores the agreement rule (except for Be verbs).

My friend lived in the city.

My friends lived in the city.

My friends are in the city.

My friends were in the city.

My friends was in the city.

5. Subjects connected by "and" take plural verbs.

Dan and Jane like jazz.

6. When subjects are connected by "or" (A or B), B is the subject.

Jane or her sisters are late. (subject = sisters)

Jane or her sisters is late.

7. EVERY, EACH, and ONE are magic words that always take singular nouns and singular verbs.

Every book has an ending.

Each book is unique.

One apple a day keeps the doctor away.

8. The subject is almost always before the preposition "of."

The king of England went to war with France. — Subject: "king"

Identifying "of" is KEY in the SAT.

9. The modifier between a Subject and a Verb does not affect the subject.

The music in Christopher Nolan's films is worth studying.

10. With many expressions of quantity, consider the noun that follows "of" as the subject.

A lot of money is required for tuition.

A lot of books contain controversial ideas.

Some of my friends have called me.

Some of the information is now available.

All research provides useful information.

Half of the world is online.

11. Either / Neither is singular.

Either is fine.    Neither is fine.

12. Either + or / Neither + nor: When a singular and a plural subject are connected, the subject closer to the verb is the main subject.

Either my brothers or my mom is angry with me.

Neither my mom nor my brothers are angry with me.

Either my brothers or my mom are angry with me.

13. A number of vs. The number of:

The number of students in my class is fifteen. ("The number" is singular.)

A number of students were late for class. ("A number of" means "many.")

14. Gerund / Infinitive subject is singular.

Watching movies is fun.

To watch a movie is fun.

15. Noun clause subject is singular.

What I want is you.

That he called me is not surprising.

Collective Nouns: In standardized grammar tests, collective nouns (nouns that refer to a group of people such as university, company, team, jury) are treated as singular.

The team has traveled.

The team have traveled.

Concept 2.2: Inversion

Sometimes, a subject doesn't always come in the beginning of a sentence. It is possible for a verb to come before the subject for EMPHASIS. This is called an inversion. In an inversion, the sentence order will be verb → subject, instead of subject → verb.

Common Inversions

1. There is/are … X (most common inversion)

There is a boy. (Verb → subject)

There are girls. (Verb → subject)

2. Prepositional phrase, or a modifier, beginning a sentence

Between two desks is a boy. (Preposition/modifier → verb → subject)

Over the rainbow were girls.

HOWEVER, when a comma is used after the modifier, inversion doesn't occur. It's just a transition:

Over the rainbow, girls were singing. (Subject → verb)

3. Negative adverbs

Never have we heard such controversial policies.

4. Beginning a Sentence with 'less/more'

Less well known are his plays written after the relationship.

Concept 5: Parallelism

Parallelism is one of the most common and important concepts tested in the SAT.

First, consider parallelism like a mirror. When words are connected by conjunctions (e.g., FANBOYS / either A or B) or idiomatic phrases (A rather than B), these words should belong to the same part of speech (noun, verb, adjective).

He is a husband and a son. (noun — noun)

She sings, writes, and dances. (verb — verb — verb)

He is not only rude but also noisy. (adjective — adjective)

Second, understand that, when writing in parallel structure, repeated words can be omitted.

I like to sing and to dance.

= I like to sing and dance. (omitting the repeated "to")

He is singing and is dancing.

= He is singing and dancing. (omitting the repeated "is")

Parallelism with Sentences

They invested more money, they fixed the problem, and they rebuilt the team.

= They invested more money, fixed the problem, and rebuilt the team. (omitting repeated "they")

How to Approach Parallelism Questions

When identifying parallelism, always check the part that is not underlined.

Parallelism basics:

When I was young, I liked to play with my friends, with my teachers, and to play with my parents.

A) No change    B) my parents    C) play with my parents    D) with my parents

Check the second part of the list (not the first) to see which word has been omitted/kept:

…with my friends, with my teachers, [C]…

The second part begins with "with" — so "to play" has been omitted and our answer must also begin with "with." The answer is D (with my parents).

Concept 6: Dangling Modifier

What is wrong with this sentence?

Walking along the river, some fish were seen by him.

The phrase "Walking along the river" is a modifier. Yet, it is placed right beside the subject "fish," as if the fish is walking. However, "fish" cannot walk. This error is called the Dangling Modifier.

To fix this error, we have to change the subject of the sentence and place the modifier as close as possible to the word it modifies.

Walking along the river, he saw some fish.

Dangling Modifier is one of the most important grammar concepts in the SAT.

Example 1:

One of the greatest plays of all time, Shakespeare wrote Othello.

Shakespeare cannot be one of the greatest plays of all time.

One of the greatest plays of all time, Othello was written by Shakespeare.

Example 2:

Jack found his science books walking to school.

Can books walk? Changing the position of the modifier is another way to fix the dangling modifier.

Walking to school, Jack found his science books.

Also, remember that modifiers don't always have to come in the beginning. With the correct usage of commas, it can appear in the middle or end of a sentence:

Walking around the classroom, the teacher motivated his students during class.

The teacher, walking around the classroom, motivated his students during class.

The teacher motivated his students during class, walking around the classroom.

Dangling Modifier simplified: Modifiers must be close to the words they modify.

For example, try to see that the bolded portions are all modifiers:

Composed by Beethoven, Moonlight Sonata is famous.

The man singing a song near the park is quite good.

The car, a gift from my father, is amazing.

I met Jane, the girl of my dreams.

Concept 6.1: Dangling Modifier Trap

Understand how these two sentences are different:

I saw Jane, walking down the street. — (I was walking, not Jane)

I saw Jane walking down the street. — (Jane was walking, not I)

Though there are exceptions, when "—ing modifier" is used with a COMMA, it GENERALLY modifies the subject of the sentence.

If a comma is not used between the noun and "—ing modifier," it modifies the word next to it.

Concept 8: Comparison

Comparison questions test our ability to make logical comparisons. The idea is simple: compare people to people and things to things.

My score is higher than Mike. (thing vs. person)

A score cannot be higher than a person.

There are three ways to fix basic comparison problems:

1. Using apostrophe

My score is higher than Mike's score.

My score is higher than Mike's.

2. Using "of"

My score is higher than the score of Mike.

3. Using THAT OF (singular) / THOSE OF (plural) — more frequently tested in SAT

Because the SAT doesn't like to repeat the same word, we can replace the repeated word with "that" or "those":

My score is higher than that of Mike.

My scores are higher than those of Mike. (plural)

Concept 8.1: Action Comparison

The SAT also tests action comparison. The sentence is not about two people: it is about the action.

I ran faster than him.

I ran faster than he ran.

I ran faster than he did.

(*do / does for present tense)

Action comparison can also be emphasized through the use of inversion:

I ran faster than he did. (s v)

I ran faster than did he. (v s) — Though this sounds awkward, it is grammatically correct and has been tested in the SAT many times.

Concept 9: Conciseness / Redundancy

"Pick the shortest answer choice when you are unsure."

To a certain extent, this advice is very helpful because conciseness/ redundancy questions appear frequently in the test. Generally, when expressing an idea, we should be clear and direct. Using unnecessary expressions or words will only make the meaning unclear.

I annually invest a large sum of money in insurance each year. (redundant: "annually" and "each year")

1. As long as the meaning doesn't change, one sentence is better than two sentences, and one verb is better than two verbs.

2. The SAT hates using the same words.

4. Passive Voice — Active sentences are considered more concise than passive sentences.

Passive voice: The rice was eaten by me.

Concept 11: Verb Tense

Essentially, there are three basic time-frames: past, present, and future. There are fundamentally 12 verb tenses in the English language, each tense expressing a different time frame.

Easy rule to remember: when you see a verb phrase (a verb with two words or more), always look at the first part of the verb.

Has studied → "has" is present → this is a present tense.

Was studying → "was" is past → this is a past tense.

Verb Tense
Has studied / Have studiedPresent
Studies / Study / Is studiedPresent
Studied / Was studying / Were studiedPast
Will studyFuture
Would have studied / Would studyPast

Concept 11.1: Important Verb Tenses

1. Simple Present

Used to show facts or something (action or event) that happens regularly. Simple Present can also be used to express the future.

I run to school every day.

Shakespeare's Romeo and Juliet portrays the theme of love.

I have a test tomorrow. (expresses future)

The use of present tense to express future time is often used with "when":

When you will hear from him, please let me know.

When you hear from him, please let me know.

2. Present Perfect (has / have + pp)

There are three different usages:

A. Used for unspecific past: I have seen snow.   I have studied.

B. To show an action that began in the past and continues in the present:

I have been married to Jane for five years.

I have loved her since 2008.

C. To show an action that began in the past and FINISHES in the present:

Scientists have long believed that X influences Y. The recent theory, however, suggests otherwise.

Signal Words: for, since, over, from

People have studied the meaning of life since the beginning of time.

I have played the violin for 8 years.

Over the last 3 years, I have prepared for a test.

From the moment I saw you, I have respected you.

3. Simple Past

Used to show a finished event in the past. A specific date or time generally requires a simple past tense.

I saw snow yesterday.

During World War I, the government changed many policies.

In 1923, people drank some weird beverages.

4. Past Perfect (had + pp)

Used to show which event happened first in the presence of other past events.

I didn't have a pencil because Jack had stolen mine. (Jack stealing = 1st event)

Signal word: by

By 1980, he had graduated from college.

By the time I arrived, he had left.

5. Would vs. Will

A. Would + verb — "Would" is the past form of "will," expressing future events in past tense.

Yesterday, I said that I would become the best scientist. (*even though it talks about the future, this is still a past tense)

B. Would + have + pp — used with conditional "IF" clauses:

If you had asked, I would have answered.

6. Conditional Tenses

Type 1: Express a condition that is very likely to happen (present + future tense).

If I pass the test, I will have a party.

Type 2: Express a condition that is unlikely or hypothetical. WERE is used for both singular and plural subjects.

If I were you, I would go home. (I am not you)

If he were here, he would tell you to stop. (He is not here)

If they had a lot of money, they would go to Canada. (they don't have much money)

Mixing Tenses

Mixing tenses is perfectly acceptable as long as it is correctly done.

Sentence Meaning
I think he likes her. He currently likes her.
I think he liked her. He used to like her, but not anymore.
I think he will like her. He will like her in the future.

How to Approach Tense Questions

Step 1: Look for parallelism in the sentence or other verbs in the paragraph.

Easy tense questions check our understanding of simple time-frames (Past, Present, Future). Look for parallelism in the sentence. If there are no other verbs, read surrounding sentences to understand the basic time-frame.

Step 2: Look for signal words of specific tense.

Medium difficulty tense questions almost always involve "present-perfect tense." Look for signal words such as "since / from" for present perfect tense (or "by" for past perfect tense).

Step 3: Understand the context and mix tenses if necessary.

The most difficult tense questions test our ability to mix different tenses. It is perfectly acceptable to mix tenses if it fits the context.

Concept 12: Adjective Clause

An adjective clause is a type of a modifier that creates some of the most difficult grammar questions in the SAT. The role of an adjective clause is simple: like any other adjective, an adjective clause modifies a noun.

The only difference between a basic adjective and an adjective clause is that the latter involves a subject and a verb.

I have a red car. (adjective)

I have a car, which is red. (adjective clause — s v)

I have a car, which I will sell. (adjective clause — s v)

People Things (including animals)
who, whom which, that

I know Jack, which is nice.

I know Jack, who is nice.

An adjective clause used to be a complete sentence, and it can be deconstructed to its original form.

Othello, which explores the theme of jealousy, is Shakespeare's masterpiece.

Deconstructed:

  1. Othello is Shakespeare's masterpiece.
  2. Othello explores the theme of jealousy.

Concept 12.1: Essential & Non-essential Adjective Clause

Essential Non-essential
that (no comma), who (no comma) ,which (comma + which), ,who (comma + who)

comma + WHICH creates a non-essential adjective clause:

Romeo and Juliet, which is about love, has a tragic ending.

Deconstructed: 1. Romeo and Juliet has a tragic ending. 2. Romeo and Juliet is about love.

THAT (without a comma) creates an essential adjective clause:

The song that Beethoven wrote for her is beautiful.

Deconstructed: 1. The song is beautiful. 2. Beethoven wrote the song for her.

For people, we use WHO and the comma differentiates essential and non-essential:

The poet who wrote "the Raven" is exceptional. (essential — no comma)

Edgar Allan Poe, who wrote "the Raven," is exceptional. (non-essential — commas)

Choosing between Essential and Non-Essential

Rule 1: Proper Noun (begins with capital letter) + non-essential modifiers

Dr. Geralt, who found the solution to the problem, contributed significantly to the development of medicine.

Rule 2: Common Noun (everyday nouns) + essential modifiers

The doctor who found the solution to the problem contributed significantly to the development of medicine.

However, when both options are given, use the rule stated above UNLESS the context demands otherwise. So far, this rule has never been broken in the SAT.

Concept 12.2: Who vs. Whom

WHO is a subject pronoun, used before a verb. WHOM is an object pronoun.

Jane, who is beautiful, is my wife.

Deconstructed: 1. Jane is my wife. 2. Jane (subject) is beautiful → "who is beautiful"

Jane, whom I met at church, is beautiful.

Deconstructed: 1. Jane is beautiful. 2. I met Jane (object) at church → "whom I met at church"

Jane, who I met at church, is beautiful.

Jane, whom I met at church, is beautiful.

I love Jane, whom is beautiful.

I love Jane, who is beautiful.

I love Jane, whom I met at church.

Concept 12.3: Whose & Where & When & Why

WHOSE: Used to show possession for both people and things.

I love Shakespeare whose plays have inspired me.

Deconstructed: 1. I love Shakespeare. 2. Shakespeare's plays have inspired me.

I bought a book whose title is unreadable.

Deconstructed: 1. I bought a book. 2. The book's title is unreadable.

WHERE: Refers to a physical place.

I want to go to Paris where people are romantic.

I like books where the ending is clear. (not a physical location)

WHEN: Refers to times and events.

I still remember the time when she called me.

WHY: Refers to reason.

I don't know the reason why she called me.

Concept 12.4: Preposition + Which/Whom

Among different types of adjective clauses, those that involve "preposition + which" or "preposition + whom" constructions are definitely the hardest.

Examples: in which, under which, by which, over which, with whom, of whom

"in which" can replace "where" on a basic level, but unlike "where," "in which" can modify both physical AND abstract locations:

I hate books where the main characters lose.

I hate books in which the main characters lose.

Concept 12.5: Deconstruction

An adjective clause used to be a complete sentence, and it can be deconstructed to its original form. To approach difficult questions with "preposition + which," we must be able to deconstruct an adjective clause.

How to Deconstruct:

I want to visit the rainbow over which angels are singing.

Step 1: Identify "preposition + which" and find the core sentence.

I want to visit the rainbow / over which angels are singing.

Step 2: Change the pronoun "which (or whom)" with the word that it is replacing.

I want to visit the rainbow / over the rainbow angels are singing.

Step 3: Place the "preposition + original word" at the end of the adjective clause.

I want to visit the rainbow / angels are singing over the rainbow.

Deconstruction Exercises

1. I looked at the water under which dolphins were dancing.

2. We should go watch a movie after which we can study.

3. The sea, beyond which she is waiting, is still far away.

4. Jack, with whom I studied, is kind.

By Which

In English, "BY" often refers to "a way of doing things" or "method." "By + which" is used in the same sense.

Better methods by which graduates can apply for jobs must be developed.

Deconstructed: 1. Better methods must be developed. 2. Graduates can apply for jobs by the method.

Learning is a process by which a person acquires new knowledge.

Deconstructed: 1. Learning is a process. 2. A person acquires new knowledge by learning.

Whereby

Whereby is a concise version of "by which" or "through which."

Grammar Breakdown, a practice whereby students learn to identify different parts of speech in a sentence, is effective.

Deconstructed: 1. Grammar Breakdown is effective. 2. Students learn to identify different parts of speech in a sentence by/through Grammar Breakdown.

Important Transitions

Below are difficult / unfamiliar transitions (and their definitions) that have appeared as an answer in the SAT test at least once or commonly in other standardized grammar tests.

Though — Arguably the most important word in the SAT. See Key Transition: Though.

In fact / Indeed — Used to emphasize the previous sentence.

Jack is very smart. In fact, he is one of the smartest boys in our school.

Conversely — Used to contrast or reverse the previous sentence (similar to "however").

Hemmingway often wrote short sentences in his novel. Conversely, Faulkner wrote long sentences.

Subsequently — Used to show that something happened AFTER the previous sentence.

A clear association between listening to classical music and memory improvement was discovered. Subsequently, many parents made their children listen to classical music.

Accordingly — Used to show a result (similar to "therefore" and "as a result").

The government increased the minimum wage. Accordingly, many people's standard of living improved.

To this end / To these ends — Definition: To achieve this goal or aim.

Jack wants to get a perfect score on his test. To this end, he must study effectively.

Given that — Definition: Considering the fact that.

Given that reading books improves vocabulary and comprehension skills, students should be encouraged to read books.

In particular — 'In particular' could mean either "specifically" or "especially." In the SAT, it has been used as "specifically."

I am interested in Shakespeare's tragedies. In particular, I am interested in Othello.

In turn — Similar to "as a result" or "because of that," used to indicate that one action leads to or causes another.

If employees are given higher incentive for their work, it will in turn motivate employees to work harder.

Then — Can be used as: (1) At that time; (2) Afterwards; (3) In that case / therefore — this usage is most important for the SAT.

They found the evidence. The case, then, is closed.

In effect — Used to summarize something; a sense that the statement is not completely accurate. Similar to "Basically."

In effect, the new system will benefit everyone.

That is (also "that is to say") — Used to clarify what has been said (similar to "In other words").

He was very considerate: that is, he would always care about what I would say.

Of course — Used to make a concession.

Watching TV is beneficial for students. Of course, some TV programs should be avoided.

Although vs. Whereas: "whereas" is used when comparing TWO things or people. "although" can be used more freely.

I went shopping, whereas I was broke.

I went shopping, although I was broke.

He likes to sleep early, whereas his wife prefers to sleep late.

In fact / Indeed vs. For example: "In fact / Indeed" are used to emphasize the first statement, while "for example" is used to give an example.

The company has released unique colors for its new laptop. In fact, these colors have never been used by any other laptops that they designed.

The company has released unique colors for its new laptop. For example, these colors have never been used by any other laptops that they designed.

How to Approach Transition Questions

Step 1: Read the answer choice before reading the passage and eliminate possible answer choices.

Use the 2:0 Rule and 3:1 Rule.

2:0 Rule: If two answer choices have the same meaning (e.g., "In addition" and "Furthermore"), cross them out because there can only be one answer per question.

3:1 Rule: When three transitions from the same group appear, the remaining answer choice is most likely the answer.

Important: "However" can replace "Nevertheless," but "Nevertheless" cannot always replace "However." Therefore, if the correct answer choice requires "contrast" transitions and you have both options, "however" will always be the answer.

Erase answer choices ONLY when you are ABSOLUTELY certain that they mean the same thing.

Step 2: Read the Sentence Before and After.

Always read the sentences before and after and find out the relationship between two sentences. Ask yourself, "Are these expressing similar ideas? Contrasting ideas?" "Is the second sentence emphasizing the first sentence?"

If you ever encounter a transition in the middle or at the end of a sentence, always bring that transition to the beginning of the sentence so that you can understand the relationship between sentences much better.

Step 3: Read the Paragraph / Topic Sentence.

80% of transition questions can be solved using the first two steps. For tricky transition questions, read the topic sentence (the first sentence of the paragraph) to understand what the paragraph is about. When the relationship between two sentences is not apparent, "addition" transitions will most likely be the answer.

KEY Transition: Though

The word "though" deserves a section of its own, for it is arguably one of the most important words in the SAT. "Though" appears in both punctuation and transition questions, and there are two different usages.

1. "Though" can be used like "Although" and "Even though," creating modifiers.

Although I liked her, she didn't.

Even though I liked her, she didn't.

Though I liked her, she didn't.

2. "Though" can be used as "However." (Most important usage for the SAT)

I went to the shop. The shop, however, was closed.

I went to the shop. The shop, though, was closed.

I went to the shop. The shop was closed, though.

*Transitions can come in the beginning, middle, and end.

Example Question:

The shop always opened at 9 am. It was 11 am when I arrived. The shop was closed, though I ran back to my car.

A) No change    B) though,    C) though;    D) though!

The correct answer is C (though;)

Answer Choice A is wrong because it is illogical (try changing 'though' to 'even though'): "The shop was closed, even though I ran back to my car." — There is no relationship between running back to the car and the store closing.

Answer Choice B creates a comma splice error (try changing 'though' to 'however'): "The shop was closed, however, I ran back to my car." — Both parts are complete sentences and cannot be connected with "however" + comma alone.

Answer Choice C fixes the comma splice error and the meaning is clear: "The shop was closed, though; I ran back to my car."

You can also see it as: Version 1: "The shop was closed, however; I ran back to my car." / Version 2: "However, the shop was closed; I ran back to my car."

Part 3: Listing / Notes Questions

Though the core grammar and the transition questions remain the same for the Digital SAT, there is a new type of question, introduced for the first time: the listing / notes question. These questions appear at the end of each module, usually 2 to 3 questions per module.

This Question is very similar to the "effectively combine questions" from the previous SAT. Before, we were given 2 sentences to combine into one sentence. Now, we are given 4–5 sentences and a very specific question. For this question, all we have to do is read the question carefully, and choose the answer choice that responds to the question in the most concise way.

We must ANSWER what the question wants. Make sure to read the question carefully. Conciseness is not important if you are not answering the question.

There are two simple but effective strategies to remember:

1. Become familiar with the questions. Based on the official tests, there are roughly 4–5 question types that are repeatedly being tested:

  • Familiar / unfamiliar
  • Differences and/or similarity
  • Significance
  • Summarize

2. Develop the habit of labeling the subject of the answer choices. So far, it seems that the answer choice whose subject echoes the topic mentioned in the question is the correct answer most of the time.

Appendix A: Parts of Speech

The words we use belong to a group known as "parts of speech." For me, there are only 6 essential parts of speech.

Part of Speech Description Examples
Noun Person, place, object, idea, or anything that has "a NAME" Daniel, Seoul, Laptop, Love
Verb A word that gives core "INFORMATION" about the subject have, go, speak, think, is
Adjective A word that MODIFIES (decorates) a noun beautiful, large, solid, wide
Adverb A word that MODIFIES a verb, adjective, other adverb, or sentence. Often ends in –ly. warmly, rapidly, very, ironically
Preposition A word that is used with another noun to show some kind of relationship or create a modifier. under, of, to, at, from
Conjunction A word that connects different words or sentences for, and, nor, but, or, yet, so

Subject and Noun

A sentence MUST have at least 2 words. A sentence must always have a Subject (the main character of a sentence) and a Verb. A SUBJECT is, in essence, always a NOUN.

A noun can be more than one word (it can even be a sentence).

6 Forms of Nouns

Form Example
i. Noun Love cures everything.
ii. Pronoun He went to the party.
iii. Noun Phrase My beautiful dress is new.
iv. Noun Clause What I wanted was you.
v. Gerund (+ phrase) Shopping is not fun.
vi. Infinitive (+ phrase) To sing is my hobby.

Verb

A verb is a word that gives primary information about the subject. Verbs can be divided into two types: moving (action) and non-moving (state).

Basic concepts regarding verbs:

  1. Verbs are divided into two time frames (tense): Present & Past
  2. Verbs are divided into two forms: Regular & Irregular (past regular verbs end with -ED; past irregular verbs have no fixed rules)
  3. Verbs are generally divided into two types: main verb & helping verb
  4. The most important but common verb in English is the BE verb
Helping Verb Example
CanI can sing.
CouldI could sing.
MayI may sing.
MightI might sing.
MustI must sing.
ShouldI should sing.
WillI will sing.
WouldI would sing.

Adjective vs. Adverb

Adjectives give more information about nouns, whereas Adverbs give more information about everything else, including the verb, sentence, and other modifiers.

Key difference: Adjectives must be close to the words they modify. Adverbs can be placed almost anywhere if they have the correct punctuation.

I have a red car. (adjective must be close)

I red have a car.

I sang a song yesterday.

Yesterday, I sang a song. (adverb can move)

Preposition

One really important concept to understand regarding a preposition is that the NOUN following the preposition CANNOT be used as the subject.

In the car is a boy.

"car" cannot become the subject because it is part of a modifier created with the preposition "in." The subject is "boy." This is an example of an inversion.

Appendix B: Verbal

A VERBAL is a word formed from a verb, used as a noun, adjective, or adverb. In other words, a verbal LOOKS like a verb, but it isn't.

I have a singing bird. (adjective)

Singing is fun. (noun)

I am singing. (verb — NOT a verbal)

Type Function Examples
1. Participle Adjective It is an interesting story. / This is a broken computer.
2. Gerund Noun Studying is fun. / I like reading.
3. Infinitive Noun, adjective, or adverb I like to study. / To read is fun. / I studied to pass the test. / The attempt to kill the king failed.

Appendix C: Modifiers (Advanced)

Participle Phrase

A participle phrase, which combines a participle with other words, functions as an adjective, and there are many types:

Present Participle Phrase — used in an active form (the subject does the action).

Walking around the classroom, the teacher motivated his students during class.

Deconstructed: 1. The teacher walked around the class. 2. The teacher motivated his student during class.

Past Participle Phrase — used in a passive form (action is done to the subject).

Made by Jane, the coffee was perfect.

Deconstructed: 1. The coffee was perfect. 2. The coffee was made by Jane.

Participle phrases don't always have to come in the beginning. With the correct usage of commas, they can appear in the middle or end of a sentence:

Walking around the classroom, the teacher motivated his students during class.

The teacher, walking around the classroom, motivated his students during class.

The teacher motivated his students during class, walking around the classroom.

Perfect Participle Phrase

Like the past perfect verb tense, this modifier shows that one action happened before another action.

Formula A: Having + a past participle (active form)

Having studied, I passed the test. (I had studied [1st event]. I passed the test [2nd].)

Formula B: Having been + a past participle (passive)

Having been captured, he was scared. (He had been captured [1st]. Then, he was scared [2nd].)

Appositive

Appositive is an adjective composed of noun(s). Because an appositive is made up of nouns, other forms of nouns can also be used to create appositives.

Jack, a boy, works hard.

Appositive, a form of an adjective composed of nouns, is frequently used.

One of the greatest musicians of all time, Charlie Parker played the saxophone.

The decision, what he had always wanted, changed his life.

I met Jane, the most beautiful girl in the world.
